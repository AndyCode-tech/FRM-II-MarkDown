## Credit Portfolios And Credit VaR

### Default Correlation for Credit Portfolios

The "standard definition" of default correlation is derived from the **Pearson Correlation Coefficient**, but applied to **Bernoulli random variables** (variables that can only be 0 or 1).

In credit risk, we treat a default as a binary event:

-   **$X = 1$** if the firm defaults.
    
-   **$X = 0$** if the firm does not default.
    

Here is the step-by-step derivation:

----------

### 1. Defining the Variables

For two firms, $A$ and $B$, we define their default indicators as $I_A$ and $I_B$.

-   The **Expected Value** of a default indicator is simply the Probability of Default (PD):
    
    $$E[I_A] = P(A)$$
    
-   The **Variance** of a Bernoulli variable is:
    
    $$Var(I_A) = P(A)(1 - P(A))$$
    

### 2. The Covariance

Correlation is defined as the Covariance divided by the product of the Standard Deviations:

$$\rho_{AB} = \frac{Cov(I_A, I_B)}{\sigma_A \sigma_B}$$

To find the **Covariance**, we use the formula $Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$:

-   $E[I_A I_B]$ is the expectation that both variables are 1 (both default). This is the **Joint Probability of Default**, $P(A \cap B)$.
    
-   $E[I_A]E[I_B]$ is the product of their individual default probabilities, $P(A)P(B)$.
    

So, the Covariance is:

$$Cov(I_A, I_B) = P(A \cap B) - P(A)P(B)$$

### 3. The Final Assembly

Plugging the Covariance and the Standard Deviations ($\sqrt{Var}$) back into the correlation formula gives you the standard expression:

$$\rho_{AB} = \frac{P(A \cap B) - P(A)P(B)}{\sqrt{P(A)(1-P(A))} \cdot \sqrt{P(B)(1-P(B))}}$$

----------

### What this tells us

-   **If $P(A \cap B) > P(A)P(B)$:** The firms are positively correlated. A default in one makes a default in the other more likely than if they were independent.
    
-   **Independence:** If the firms are independent, $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$, and the numerator becomes zero, resulting in a correlation of **0**.
    
-   **The Constraint:** Unlike asset returns, default correlation is mathematically capped. Because $P(A \cap B)$ cannot exceed the probability of the weaker firm defaulting, the correlation often cannot reach $+1$ unless both firms have the exact same PD.
    
 ## Credit Portfolio Framework 
 ### Drawbacks in using the correlation-based credit portfolio framework
This is a classic critique of the **Default Correlation** framework, often discussed in the context of why the industry moved toward **Copula** or **Factor models**.

Here is a summary of the four major drawbacks you provided:

----------

## 1. The "Curse of Dimensionality" (Mathematical Complexity)

The number of possible outcomes grows exponentially as you add more firms to a portfolio.

-   **The Math:** For $n$ firms, there are $2^n$ possible default combinations.
    
-   **The Problem:** A 10-firm portfolio requires tracking 1,024 outcomes, which becomes computationally impossible for large bank portfolios.
    
-   **The Shortcut:** To cope, analysts often oversimplify by assuming a single correlation parameter for everyone, which sacrifices accuracy.
    

## 2. Incompatibility with Complex Instruments

The framework is too "binary" (default vs. no-default) to handle modern financial products.

-   **Option-like features:** It fails to capture the nuances of guarantees, revolving credit, or contingent liabilities.
    
-   **Hybrid Risks:** Convertible bonds (which act like both debt and equity) and CDS basis trades don't fit into a simple correlation bucket.
    

## 3. Ignorance of "Technical Factors"

The model assumes credit risk is driven purely by the health of the firm, but market mechanics often take over during a crisis.

-   **Liquidity Risk:** During the subprime crisis, assets didn't just fail because of credit; they failed because there were no buyers. Simple correlation models do not account for a sudden "vanishing" of market liquidity.
    

## 4. The Data Scarcity Problem

Correlation is notoriously difficult to estimate because defaults are **"rare events."**

-   **Estimation Noise:** Because firms rarely go bankrupt, the "sample size" is tiny. This makes correlation estimates highly sensitive to the time period or industry chosen.
    
-   **The "0.05" Rule:** Due to this lack of data, many models default to a tiny correlation (like **0.05**), which may dangerously underestimate the risk of a "domino effect" during a systemic crash.
    

----------

### Comparison: Default Correlation vs. Asset Correlation

To solve the "1,024 outcomes" problem mentioned in your text, most banks switch to **Asset Correlation** (Factor Models).

| Feature | Default Correlation (Your Text) | Asset Correlation (The Alternative) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Calculation** | $2^n$ outcomes (Exponential) | $N$ calculations (Linear) |
| **Data Source** | Rare default events | Daily stock/market returns |
| **Logic** | Did they both fail? | Do their values move together? |

## Credit VaR
$\text{Credit VaR}_{\alpha} = \text{Worst-Case Loss}_{\alpha} - \text{Expected Loss}$

Where:

-   **$\alpha$ (Alpha):** The confidence level (typically $99\%$ or $99.9\%$).
    
-   **Worst-Case Loss$_{\alpha}$:** The percentile of the loss distribution corresponding to $1 - \alpha$.
    
-   **Expected Loss (EL):** The loss the institution anticipates on average.
    

> **Why subtract Expected Loss?** > Financial institutions already reserve capital or price loans to cover _Expected Losses_. Therefore, Credit VaR is designed to capture only **Unexpected Loss (UL)**—the risk that capital buffers must absorb.

## Conditional Default Probabilities and Credit VaR with Copulas

Here is the comprehensive, structured summary of the full single-factor credit model, explaining its mechanics, the concept of conditional independence, and how market shifts alter default risk.

----------

## 1. Core Model & Asset Returns

The single-factor model splits a firm’s asset return into **systemic (market) risk** and **idiosyncratic (firm-specific) risk**. For any firm $i$, the return ($a_i$) is expressed as:

$$a_i = \beta_i m + \sqrt{1 - \beta_i^2} \varepsilon_i$$

-   **$m$ (Market Factor):** Represents overall economic health; a standard normal variate.
    
-   **$\beta_i$ (Beta):** The firm's correlation with the market. Consequently, the asset correlation between any two firms $i$ and $j$ is simply $\beta_i \beta_j$.
    
-   **$\varepsilon_i$ (Idiosyncratic Shock):** Uncorrelated, firm-specific risk.
----------

## 2. Default Condition & Conditional Independence

-   **The Default Threshold:** A firm defaults if its asset return falls below a critical value ($a_i \le k_i$), where $k_i$ is the logarithmic distance to default.
    
-   **Conditional Independence:** Once the market factor ($m$) is realized and fixed, **individual default risks become entirely independent**. Because the remaining risk is purely idiosyncratic ($\varepsilon_i$), one firm defaulting does not inherently cause another to default. This assumption vastly simplifies portfolio credit risk calculations.
    

----------

## 3. Shifting from Unconditional to Conditional Credit Risk

When we evaluate the model under a specific market state ($m = \bar{m}$), the statistical distribution of the firm's asset return shifts from its baseline **unconditional** state to a **conditional** state:
| Metric | Unconditional Distribution | Conditional Distribution (Given $m = \bar{m}$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Distribution Type** | Standard Normal | Normal |
| **Mean (Expected Return)** | $0$ | $\beta_i \bar{m}$ |
| **Standard Deviation (Risk)**| $1$ | $\sqrt{1 - \beta_i^2}$ |

----------

## 4. Key Implications of Market Movements

Fixing the market factor to a specific value yields three major insights regarding default probabilities:

-   **Shifting Default Probabilities:** The conditional probability of default will deviate from the unconditional baseline based on economic health. The new criteria for default relies entirely on the idiosyncratic shock:
    
    $$\varepsilon_i \le \frac{k_i - \beta_i \bar{m}}{\sqrt{1 - \beta_i^2}}$$
    
-   **The Economic Cycle Effect:** * In a **strong economy** ($\bar{m} > 0$), the distance to default expands. It requires a massive, highly negative idiosyncratic shock ($\varepsilon_i$) to trigger a default.
    
    -   In a **weak economy** ($\bar{m} < 0$), the mean shifts downward. The distance to default shrinks, meaning even a minor negative firm-specific shock can trigger a default.
        
-   **Reduced Volatility:** The conditional standard deviation ($\sqrt{1 - \beta_i^2}$) is **always less than 1** (the unconditional volatility). This is because the uncertainty tied to the broader economy has already been resolved and removed from the equation.

## Appendix 
Here is the complete, step-by-step derivation and explanation of the **Single-Factor Credit Risk Model** (often called the **Vasicek Model**).

This framework is the mathematical backbone used by global banks and regulators (like Basel III/IV) to calculate portfolio credit risk and regulatory capital.

----------

## 1. The Core Concept: Systematic vs. Idiosyncratic Risk

The model simplifies a complex portfolio of thousands of borrowers by assuming that the financial health or asset value change of any single borrower ($X_i$) is driven by only two forces:

1.  **The Systematic Factor ($Y$):** The overall state of the economy (GDP, interest rates, etc.). This impacts **all** borrowers simultaneously.
    
2.  **The Idiosyncratic Factor ($Z_i$):** Company-specific events (e.g., a factory fire, a bad management decision). This is entirely unique to borrower $i$ and independent of the economy.
    

To use this in probability statistics, we require all variables to be **standard normal distributions** ($\sim N(0,1)$), meaning they have a mean of 0 and a total variance (risk) of 1.

----------

## 2. Part I: Deriving the Asset Value Equation

We start by assuming that a borrower’s asset return ($X_i$) is a linear combination of the economy ($Y$) and company risk ($Z_i$), using two unknown weights, $a$ and $b$:

$$X_i = aY + bZ_i$$

### Step A: Finding $a$ (Using Asset Correlation)

If we look at two different companies in a portfolio ($X_i$ and $X_j$), their unique risks ($Z_i$ and $Z_j$) are completely independent. Therefore, any correlation between the two companies is driven entirely by their shared exposure to the economy ($Y$).

Let $\rho$ (Rho) be the defined asset correlation between these two firms. Since the variables are standardized, correlation equals covariance:

$$\rho = Cov(X_i, X_j)$$

$$\rho = Cov(aY + bZ_i, \ aY + bZ_j)$$

Expanding this using the algebraic properties of covariance yields:

$$\rho = Cov(aY, aY) + Cov(aY, bZ_j) + Cov(bZ_i, aY) + Cov(bZ_i, bZ_j)$$

Because $Y, Z_i,$ and $Z_j$ are mutually independent, all cross-covariance terms collapse to zero, leaving only the first term:

$$\rho = Cov(aY, aY)$$

$$\rho = a^2 Var(Y)$$

Since the economy factor is standardized ($Var(Y) = 1$), this simplifies to:

$$\rho = a^2 \implies a = \sqrt{\rho}$$

### Step B: Finding $b$ (Using Total Variance)

Now we substitute $a = \sqrt{\rho}$ back into our baseline equation: $X_i = \sqrt{\rho}Y + bZ_i$.

Because we require the total variance of the borrower's asset return to equal 1 ($Var(X_i) = 1$), we calculate the variance of both sides:

$$Var(X_i) = Var(\sqrt{\rho}Y + bZ_i)$$

$$1 = (\sqrt{\rho})^2 Var(Y) + b^2 Var(Z_i)$$

Since $Var(Y) = 1$ and $Var(Z_i) = 1$:

$$1 = \rho + b^2 \implies b^2 = 1 - \rho \implies b = \sqrt{\1 - \rho}$$

### The Completed Asset Value Equation

Combining our values for $a$ and $b$ gives us the foundational equation:

$$X_i = \sqrt{\rho} Y + \sqrt{1 - \rho} Z_i$$

_This setup is structurally elegant: the systematic variance ($\rho$) plus the idiosyncratic variance ($1 - \rho$) always perfectly sums to 1._

----------

## 3. Part II: Deriving the Vasicek Conditional PD Formula

Now that we have the asset equation, we can determine the probability that a borrower will default given a specific state of the economy.

### Step A: Defining the Unconditional Default Threshold

The model states that a borrower defaults if their asset value ($X_i$) falls below a critical financial threshold, $C_i$. The _unconditional_ probability of default ($PD_i$) is:

$$PD_i = P(X_i < C_i)$$

Because $X_i$ follows a standard normal distribution, we can map this probability using the standard normal cumulative distribution function, $\Phi(\cdot)$:

$$PD_i = \Phi(C_i)$$

To find the exact asset value cutoff point ($C_i$), we take the inverse function:

$$C_i = \Phi^{-1}(PD_i)$$

### Step B: Conditioning on the Economy

What happens to the borrower if we experience a specific economic scenario? Let's fix the market factor $Y$ to a specific known value, $y$.

If $Y = y$ is no longer a random variable but a known constant, the default condition ($X_i < C_i$) becomes:

$$\sqrt{\rho} y + \sqrt{1 - \rho} Z_i < C_i$$

### Step C: Isolating Company-Specific Risk ($Z_i$)

Since $Z_i$ is now the only random variable left in the equation, we isolate it on the left side:

$$\sqrt{1 - \rho} Z_i < C_i - \sqrt{\rho} y$$

$$Z_i < \frac{C_i - \sqrt{\rho} y}{\sqrt{1 - \rho}}$$

### Step D: The Final Substitution

The **Conditional Probability of Default**—written as $p_i(y)$—is the probability that $Z_i$ drops below this calculated boundary.

Because $Z_i \sim N(0,1)$, we pass this boundary right back into the standard normal CDF ($\Phi$):

$$p_i(y) = \Phi\left( \frac{C_i - \sqrt{\rho} y}{\sqrt{1 - \rho}} \right)$$

Finally, substituting our threshold definition from Step A ($C_i = \Phi^{-1}(PD_i)$) gives us the definitive **Vasicek Single-Factor Conditional Default Formula**:

$$p_i(y) = \Phi\left( \frac{\Phi^{-1}(PD_i) - \sqrt{\rho} y}{\sqrt{1 - \rho}} \right)$$

----------

## Summary of How It Works in Practice

Risk managers utilize this final formula to stress-test loan portfolios:

-   **In a Boom ($y > 0$):** The term $-\sqrt{\rho}y$ subtracts from the numerator, lowering the conditional default rate well below the baseline $PD_i$.
    
-   **In a Recession ($y < 0$):** The double negative ($-\sqrt{\rho}(-y)$) adds to the numerator. This drives the conditional default rate up.
    

By assuming that all loans are independent _once the economy ($y$) is fixed_, banks can easily simulate worst-case economic scenarios (e.g., setting $y = -2.33$ for a 99% worst-case year) to determine exactly how much cash reserve they need to survive a systemic crash.
