# Sovereign Default Risk
- Holders of government-issued debt fail to receive the full amount of promised interest and principal payment during the specified time period.
- Sovereign Default Risk can be used as a proxy for country risk
- Foreign currency defaults and local currency defaults

## Foreign Currency Defaults 
- Borrowed money from other countries or banks
- The government is not having enough foreign currency and unable to print money in a foreign currency to repay the debt
- Countries were more likely to default on funds borrowed from banks than on sovereign bond issues.
- Latin America accounts for a large proportion of sovereign defaults in the last 50 years (measured in dollar value terms)

## Local Currency Defaults
- If local defaults --> oftern foreign default
Factors explaining local currency defaults
- Gold standard 
	- need to have gold to back up the currency
- Shared Currency
	- unable to print money 
	- example: EU
- The tradeoff 
	- printing money --> devalue and debase the currency
	- Higher inflation
	- What extent foreign currency debt is funding local currency assets
		- the greater the extent --> more negative the consequences
		- Reducing asset values while leavinf liabilities unchanged 

## Consequences of Sovereign Default
- Defaults were often followed by military actions
- Loss of reputation --> difficult and more expensive to borrow in the future
- Turmoil in stock and bond markets
- Decrease in real output and deal with political instability
- Default Result
	- Restructuring of debt
	- GDP decline - 0.5% to -2.0% in the first year
	- Lower sovereign ratings and higher borrowing costs
	- Trade retaliation: Drop in 8% bilateral trade, last up to 15 years
	- More fragile banking systems: 14% prob banking crisis, normal is 3%
	- Increased likelihood of political change

## Factors Influencing Sovereign Default Risk
1. Level of Indebtedness
	- Most fundamental 
	- debts to foreign banks and investors and amount the country owes its own citizens (social safety, welfare, universal health)
2. Pension funds and social services 
	- Greater persion commitments and health care commitments --> higher default risk 
	- These commitments increase as the population agres 
	- Countries with older populations face greater risks
3. Tax receipts
	- more tax receipts = more income = more revenues = lower default risk
4. Stable of tax receipts
	 - Countries wil more diversified economies are more likely to have stable tax receipts 
	 - diversified economies =  more industries
5. Political risk
	- more independent the central bank --> difficult for conuntry to print money
6. Backing from other countries
	- Stronger economies will protect weaker economies against default 
	- not a guarantee but an implicit backing

## Shortcomings of the Sovereign Rating System
- Ratings are biased upward
	- Conflict of interest
- Herd behaviour (群體行為)
	- one agency upgrades or downgrades a country, the other agencies tend to follow suit
	- lack of independence reduces the beneit of having three rating agencies
- Rating changes are not timely enough:
	- Taking too long to change ratings --> leaving investors unprotected in the event of a crisis
- Overreaction leads to a vicious cycle
	- lowering ratings too much in response to a crisis, which then worsens the crisis
- Ratings failures
	- multiple ratings changes in a short period --> incorrect in their initial assessments of the country
	- Bad information: Government may hide the truth and reveal only positive information
	- Limited resources: 
		- Rating sovereigns generate limited revenues for agencies
		- Rely on common information available in the market rather than doing their own research and may also contribute to herd behavior.

	- Revenue bias: 
		- Rating agencies generate signi icant revenues from subsovereigns (States, provinces, cities)
		- An incentive for the agency to refrain from issuing harsh judgments against a country
		- A sovereign ratings downgrade is usually followed by a series of subsovereign downgrades

	- Other conflicts of interest
		- Off-shoot businesses from the core sovereign ratings business
		- employees do not go work for the sovereigns they rate, thus, this issue does not generally pose a conlict of interest.

## Credit Ratings, Default Spreads and CDS Spreads
- Sovereign default spread:
	- Difference between the yield of a riskier sovereign bond yield and a riskless sovereign bond yield (UST)
- Pros
	- Market-based spreads are more dynamic than ratings
	- Spreads adjust quicker to new information
- Cons
	- Comparing local currency bond yields is less meaningful
		- No risk-free security to compare
		- Difference in yields of other countries lock currency bonds reflecting the inflation
		- UST needed to risk-free in assumption 
	- Greater volatility in default spreads
		- Spreads may be affected by unrelated default risk variable such as demand, liquidity 
- Default spreads are positively correlated with rating and with default
- Default spreads are "leading indicatros"
	- Will widen before downgrade and narrow before upgrade
- A rating change provides information to the market, despite the longer lag time relative to default spreads
	- Ratings and default spreads are useful to market participants when evaluating and understanding sovereign default risk.

### CDS Spreads
- Mechanism 
	-  CDS buyer pays periodic payments as a percentage of the bond notional value in each period to CDS seller
	- CDS seller providers protection that in a perdefined credit event (default or restructuring
	- Settlement can be cash-based in difference between the bond's notional value and its market price 
	- Or delivers the bond the CDS seller and receives the notional value in return
	- Example:
		- An investor may own $20 million notional value ive-year Peruvian government bonds. If the investor is worried about a potential credit event over the next ive years, it could buy a ive-year CDS and pay 170 basis points (1.70%), or $340,000, annually to a CDS seller. The percentage (i.e., number of basis points) of notional value is known as the CDS spread. If a credit event such as a default or restructuring occurs over the next ive years, the CDS buyer will either deliver the bond to the seller and receive $20 million, or receive from the seller the difference between the notional value and market value of the bond.

- Key Risk in CDS protection
	- A credit event must occur (if the bond value declines due to other reasons, no credit event occurred --> no payment from the seller
	- Buyer is exposed to the credit risk that the seller may not make a payment

-  CDS spread 
	- The percentage (i.e., number of basis points) of notional value need to pay to seller
	- Adjust more quickly to market information than rating.

- Conclusions in change in CDS spread
	1. Changes in CDS spreads influence sovereign bond yields and ratings. 
	2. CDS spreads are better predictors of sovereign risk than credit ratings.
	3. Increased macroeconomic risks result in increases in CDS spreads, which are also often accompanied by currency depreciations.
	4. Contagion (or clustering) leads CDS spreads of groups of countries to move together.

- Limitations
	- CDS spread reflect  a range of risk such as credit, market, liquidity risk --> Using CDS to estimate credit risk will be overestimated
	- CDSs are illiquid --> distorts their prices
