# Sovereign Default Risk
- Holders of government-issued debt fail to receive the full amount of promised interest and principal payment during the specified time period.
- Sovereign Default Risk can be used as a proxy for country risk
- Foreign currency defaults and local currency defaults

## Foreign Currency Defaults 
- Borrowed money from other countries or banks
- The government is not having enough foreign currency and unable to print money in a foreign currency to repay the debt
- Countries were more likely to default on funds borrowed from banks than on sovereign bond issues.
- Latin America accounts for a large proportion of sovereign defaults in the last 50 years (measured in dollar value terms)

## Local Currency Defaults
- If local defaults --> oftern foreign default
Factors explaining local currency defaults
- Gold standard 
	- need to have gold to back up the currency
- Shared Currency
	- unable to print money 
	- example: EU
- The tradeoff 
	- printing money --> devalue and debase the currency
	- Higher inflation
	- What extent foreign currency debt is funding local currency assets
		- the greater the extent --> more negative the consequences
		- Reducing asset values while leavinf liabilities unchanged 

## Consequences of Sovereign Default
- Defaults were often followed by military actions
- Loss of reputation --> difficult and more expensive to borrow in the future
- Turmoil in stock and bond markets
- Decrease in real output and deal with political instability
- Default Result
	- Restructuring of debt
	- GDP decline - 0.5% to -2.0% in the first year
	- Lower sovereign ratings and higher borrowing costs
	- Trade retaliation: Drop in 8% bilateral trade, last up to 15 years
	- More fragile banking systems: 14% prob banking crisis, normal is 3%
	- Increased likelihood of political change

## Factors Influencing Sovereign Default Risk
1. Level of Indebtedness
	- Most fundamental 
	- debts to foreign banks and investors and amount the country owes its own citizens (social safety, welfare, universal health)
2. Pension funds and social services 
	- Greater persion commitments and health care commitments --> higher default risk 
	- These commitments increase as the population agres 
	- Countries with older populations face greater risks
3. Tax receipts
	- more tax receipts = more income = more revenues = lower default risk
4. Stable of tax receipts
	 - Countries wil more diversified economies are more likely to have stable tax receipts 
	 - diversified economies =  more industries
5. Political risk
	- more independent the central bank --> difficult for conuntry to print money
6. Backing from other countries
	- Stronger economies will protect weaker economies against default 
	- not a guarantee but an implicit backing

### How Rating Agencies Measure Risk
- Rating measure
	- S&P's --> probability of default 
	- Moody;s --> probability of default and the severity of the default

- Evaluating factors that may contribute to default

		- country’s external debt burden
	- Rating Process
		- Draft report
		- Committee by 5- 10 ppl --> debate the rating
		- Vote
	- Local vs. foreign currency ratings
		- Difference based on **monetary policy independence**
		- Countries that have given up monetary policy independence (e.g., EU countries) will see their foreign and local currency ratings converge
		- Countries with loating rate exchange regimes that fund borrowing through sound domestic markets will have the greatest difference between the two ratings
		- Notch-up approach: based on domestic debt market and other domestic factors
		- Notch-down approach: based on foreign exchange issues and constraints.
	- Ratings review:
		- On a periodic basis
