# Estimating Default Probabilities

## Credit Ratings and Default Probabilities
### **1. Public Rating Agencies (Large Issuers)**

-   **Purpose:** Agencies provide credit ratings to the public as indicators of credit quality.
    
-   **Stability over Volatility:** To avoid frequent changes based on short-term market noise, agencies prioritize **stability** and take a **long-term view**.
    
-   **"Rating Through the Cycle":** Agencies look past temporary fluctuations. A short-term increase in default risk may not trigger a rating change if the five-year outlook remains stable.
    

### **2. Internal Rating Systems (Small- to Mid-Sized Firms)**

-   **The Gap:** Because agencies rarely rate smaller firms, lenders develop their own **internal credit rating systems**.
    
-   **Key Metrics:** These systems evaluate a firm based on three pillars:
    
    -   **Profitability** (e.g., Return on Equity)
        
    -   **Liquidity** (e.g., Quick Ratio)
        
    -   **Solvency** (e.g., Debt-to-Equity)
        

### **3. The Importance of Cash Flow**

-   **Repayment Ability:** Since debt must be settled with cash, lenders adjust standard financial statements to create a **cash flow statement**.
    
-   **Primary Tool:** The cash flow statement is considered the most essential tool for determining a firm’s actual ability to repay its loans.

## Borrowing Rating vs. Probability of Default

### 1. The Rating-Risk Relationship

The fundamental rule of borrowing is that credit ratings and default risk are inversely related:

-   **Lower ratings** correlate with a **higher** probability of default.
    
-   **Higher ratings** correlate with a **lower** probability of default.
    

### 2. Investment-Grade Trends (e.g., AA Rating)

For high-quality issuers, the marginal probability of default typically **increases** during the initial years.

-   **The Logic:** Issuers are assumed stable at the start, but the potential for financial deterioration grows as time passes.
    
-   **Example:** A bond rated **AA** sees its default risk climb from 0.02% in Year 1 to 0.05% by Year 3.
    

### 3. Non-Investment-Grade Trends (e.g., B Rating)

For lower-quality "junk" bonds, the marginal probability of default may eventually **decrease** after a critical initial period.

-   **The Logic:** The first few years are a "survival" phase. If the issuer avoids default early on, their financial state is viewed as less precarious than initially feared.
    
-   **Example:** A bond rated **B** peaks at a 4.46% default risk in Year 2 but shows a consistent **declining trend** in Years 3 and 4.

| Bond Category | Initial Assumption | Default Probability Trend |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Investment-Grade (AA)** | Stable at the outset | **Increases** over time as potential financial deterioration occurs. |
| **Non-Investment-Grade (B)** | Precarious / Critical survival | **Decreases** over time once the initial survival period has passed. |

## Recovery Rates

### 1. The Concept of Recovery Rate

When an issuer defaults, creditors rarely receive full repayment. The **recovery rate** is defined as the bond's trading price (as a fraction of its face value) roughly one month after the default occurs.

### 2. Seniority and Recovery

The likelihood of repayment is directly tied to the bond's ranking in the capital structure:

-   **Senior Bonds:** Have higher recovery rates (e.g., **First Lien Bonds** at **54.6%**).
    
-   **Junior Bonds:** Have lower recovery rates (e.g., **Subordinated Bonds** at **32.1%**).
    

### 3. The Inverse Relationship (Negative Correlation)

There is a **negative correlation** between default rates and recovery rates, meaning as one goes up, the other tends to go down.

-   **In a Strong Economy:** Default rates are low, and recovery rates are high.
    
-   **In a Weak Economy:** Default rates are high, and recovery rates are low.
    

### 4. The "Lender’s Challenge"

Lenders face a "double hit" during economic downturns. A weak economy creates a surplus of defaulted assets (like foreclosed homes or liquidated company assets) on the market. This oversupply drives down prices, simultaneously increasing the **frequency** of defaults while decreasing the **amount** recovered from each.

## Credit Default Swaps

A **Credit Default Swap (CDS)** is a credit derivative where a protection buyer makes periodic payments to a protection seller to hedge against the risk of a "reference entity" defaulting.

### 1. The Mechanics of a CDS

-   **The Buyer:** Seeks protection and pays a **fixed quarterly spread** (premium) to the seller.
    
-   **The Seller:** Provides protection and receives the premium. They are only required to pay out if a **credit event** occurs (e.g., bankruptcy, debt restructuring, or non-payment).
    
-   **Payments:** Premiums are paid until the contract matures or a credit event occurs. If a default happens between payment dates, the buyer makes a final **accrual payment** to cover the time since the last payment.
    

### 2. Settlement Methods

If a credit event occurs, the contract is settled in one of two ways:

-   **Physical Delivery:** The seller pays the buyer the full **face value** (notional principal) of the debt, and the buyer delivers the actual defaulted bonds to the seller.
    
    -   _Note:_ Buyers often deliver the **Cheapest-to-Deliver (CTD)** bond from a list of acceptable securities.
        
-   **Cash Settlement:** The seller pays the buyer the difference between the face value and the current market value of the bonds (determined by dealer surveys).
    

### 3. Practical Example: ELF Corp Bond

Using the figures provided in the text:

-   **Notional Principal:** $200 million.
    
-   **CDS Spread:** 75 basis points (0.75%).
    
-   **Annual Cost:** $1.5 million ($375,000 per quarter).
    
-   **Outcome if Default occurs:** If the bond drops to $0.40 on the dollar, a cash settlement would pay the buyer **60%** of the notional value, or **$120 million**.
    

### 4. Standard Terms and Maturities

-   **Common Maturities:** Range from 1 to 10 years, with **5 years** being the industry standard.
    
-   **Maturity Dates:** Contracts use standard dates (March 20, June 20, Sept 20, Dec 20).
    
-   **Accrual Adjustments:** Because of these fixed dates, the first and last payments are often adjusted (accrued) to account for the exact timing of the contract's initiation or the credit event.

### Overview of Key Credit Indices

The two primary investment-grade credit indices are the **CDX NA IG** (North America) and the **iTraxx Europe**. Each index consists of **125 reference entities**, with common maturities of 3, 5, 7, and 10 years (typically settling on June 20 and December 20).

----------

### Payment Structure and Mechanics

The cost of protection is determined by the index quote (bid/ask spread) and the amount of protection sought per firm.

**Example: 5-year iTraxx Europe (Bid 150 bps / Ask 152 bps)**

-   **Buying Protection:** Using the ask price of 152 bps, an investor seeking €500,000 of protection per firm would make total annual payments of **€950,000** ($0.0152 \times €500,000 \times 125$).
    
-   **Selling Protection:** Using the bid price of 150 bps, an investor would receive total annual receipts of **€937,500** ($0.015 \times €500,000 \times 125$).
    

----------

### Impact of Default

If a purchase of protection is in place and one reference entity defaults:

1.  **Payoff:** The investor receives the appropriate payoff for that specific entity.
    
2.  **Premium Reduction:** Future annual payments are reduced by **€7,600**, which represents the proportional share of the total premium attributed to that single entity ($€950,000 / 125$).
