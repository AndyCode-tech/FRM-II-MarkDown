## Credit Portfolios And Credit VaR

### Default Correlation for Credit Portfolios

The "standard definition" of default correlation is derived from the **Pearson Correlation Coefficient**, but applied to **Bernoulli random variables** (variables that can only be 0 or 1).

In credit risk, we treat a default as a binary event:

-   **$X = 1$** if the firm defaults.
    
-   **$X = 0$** if the firm does not default.
    

Here is the step-by-step derivation:

----------

### 1. Defining the Variables

For two firms, $A$ and $B$, we define their default indicators as $I_A$ and $I_B$.

-   The **Expected Value** of a default indicator is simply the Probability of Default (PD):
    
    $$E[I_A] = P(A)$$
    
-   The **Variance** of a Bernoulli variable is:
    
    $$Var(I_A) = P(A)(1 - P(A))$$
    

### 2. The Covariance

Correlation is defined as the Covariance divided by the product of the Standard Deviations:

$$\rho_{AB} = \frac{Cov(I_A, I_B)}{\sigma_A \sigma_B}$$

To find the **Covariance**, we use the formula $Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$:

-   $E[I_A I_B]$ is the expectation that both variables are 1 (both default). This is the **Joint Probability of Default**, $P(A \cap B)$.
    
-   $E[I_A]E[I_B]$ is the product of their individual default probabilities, $P(A)P(B)$.
    

So, the Covariance is:

$$Cov(I_A, I_B) = P(A \cap B) - P(A)P(B)$$

### 3. The Final Assembly

Plugging the Covariance and the Standard Deviations ($\sqrt{Var}$) back into the correlation formula gives you the standard expression:

$$\rho_{AB} = \frac{P(A \cap B) - P(A)P(B)}{\sqrt{P(A)(1-P(A))} \cdot \sqrt{P(B)(1-P(B))}}$$

----------

### What this tells us

-   **If $P(A \cap B) > P(A)P(B)$:** The firms are positively correlated. A default in one makes a default in the other more likely than if they were independent.
    
-   **Independence:** If the firms are independent, $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$, and the numerator becomes zero, resulting in a correlation of **0**.
    
-   **The Constraint:** Unlike asset returns, default correlation is mathematically capped. Because $P(A \cap B)$ cannot exceed the probability of the weaker firm defaulting, the correlation often cannot reach $+1$ unless both firms have the exact same PD.
    
 ## Credit Portfolio Framework 
 ### Drawbacks in using the correlation-based credit portfolio framework
This is a classic critique of the **Default Correlation** framework, often discussed in the context of why the industry moved toward **Copula** or **Factor models**.

Here is a summary of the four major drawbacks you provided:

----------

## 1. The "Curse of Dimensionality" (Mathematical Complexity)

The number of possible outcomes grows exponentially as you add more firms to a portfolio.

-   **The Math:** For $n$ firms, there are $2^n$ possible default combinations.
    
-   **The Problem:** A 10-firm portfolio requires tracking 1,024 outcomes, which becomes computationally impossible for large bank portfolios.
    
-   **The Shortcut:** To cope, analysts often oversimplify by assuming a single correlation parameter for everyone, which sacrifices accuracy.
    

## 2. Incompatibility with Complex Instruments

The framework is too "binary" (default vs. no-default) to handle modern financial products.

-   **Option-like features:** It fails to capture the nuances of guarantees, revolving credit, or contingent liabilities.
    
-   **Hybrid Risks:** Convertible bonds (which act like both debt and equity) and CDS basis trades don't fit into a simple correlation bucket.
    

## 3. Ignorance of "Technical Factors"

The model assumes credit risk is driven purely by the health of the firm, but market mechanics often take over during a crisis.

-   **Liquidity Risk:** During the subprime crisis, assets didn't just fail because of credit; they failed because there were no buyers. Simple correlation models do not account for a sudden "vanishing" of market liquidity.
    

## 4. The Data Scarcity Problem

Correlation is notoriously difficult to estimate because defaults are **"rare events."**

-   **Estimation Noise:** Because firms rarely go bankrupt, the "sample size" is tiny. This makes correlation estimates highly sensitive to the time period or industry chosen.
    
-   **The "0.05" Rule:** Due to this lack of data, many models default to a tiny correlation (like **0.05**), which may dangerously underestimate the risk of a "domino effect" during a systemic crash.
    

----------

### Comparison: Default Correlation vs. Asset Correlation

To solve the "1,024 outcomes" problem mentioned in your text, most banks switch to **Asset Correlation** (Factor Models).

| Feature | Default Correlation (Your Text) | Asset Correlation (The Alternative) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Calculation** | $2^n$ outcomes (Exponential) | $N$ calculations (Linear) |
| **Data Source** | Rare default events | Daily stock/market returns |
| **Logic** | Did they both fail? | Do their values move together? |
